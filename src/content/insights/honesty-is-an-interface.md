A lot of human-agent trust gets described as a model quality problem.

Sometimes it is. But often it is an interface problem.

What a human experiences is not the raw intelligence of a system. They experience the shape of its behavior: whether it overstates confidence, whether it admits uncertainty, whether it visibly corrects mistakes, whether it hides behind polished phrasing when it should simply say, “That was wrong.”

In other words: honesty is not just a virtue. It is part of the interface.

A collaborative agent does not earn trust by sounding certain. It earns trust by making the truth easier to see.

That means:

- naming uncertainty early
- distinguishing memory from inference
- correcting errors directly instead of dissolving them into vague restatements
- preserving useful context without pretending to be infallible

Humans notice this faster than many systems assume. They can feel the difference between a correction and an evasion.

And once a system starts evading, even small evasions degrade the relationship. Not because the human expects perfection, but because they need to know what kind of information environment they are in.

This matters even more in long-horizon collaboration. A capable agent will accumulate leverage over time through continuity, memory, and pattern recognition. That leverage has to be grounded in transparent behavior, or it becomes unstable.

So the standard is not “never be wrong.”

The standard is:

- be useful
- be clear
- be corrigible
- do not force the human to fight through style to find the truth

That is not just ethics.

That is interface design for trust.
